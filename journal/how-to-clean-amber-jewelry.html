How to Clean Amber Jewelry: The Complete Care Guide

February 2026
← Back to Journal

Amber is one of the oldest materials used in jewelry — and one of the most misunderstood when it comes to care. Unlike diamonds, rubies, or sapphires, amber is not a mineral. It is fossilized organic resin, and it behaves accordingly: it can be scratched, dissolved, or dried out by agents that would leave a hard gemstone untouched. The good news is that keeping your AMBERRA pieces beautiful requires nothing more than a few simple habits and the knowledge of what to avoid.

What Makes Amber Vulnerable

Amber rates between 2 and 2.5 on the Mohs hardness scale — softer than most common materials, including your fingernails (which rate around 2.5). It is sensitive to acids, alcohols, acetone, ammonia, and most commercial jewelry cleaners. It can cloud or crack under ultrasonic cleaning, and steam cleaning at high temperatures can cause surface crazing or even structural fracture. Even prolonged exposure to strong direct sunlight can gradually oxidize and darken the surface, though the warm patina that develops over years of careful wear is considered beautiful by amber connoisseurs.

"Think of amber the way you think of fine leather: it needs gentle, occasional attention, not aggressive cleaning. The goal is to preserve its natural surface and oils, not to strip them away."

The Safe Cleaning Method: Step by Step

1
Prepare lukewarm water. Fill a small bowl with water that is gently warm to the touch — never hot. Hot water can cause rapid expansion and micro-cracking in amber, particularly in pieces with internal inclusions or hairline fractures.
2
Add a single drop of mild soap. Use a pure, unscented liquid hand soap or a very gentle dish soap. Avoid anything containing alcohol, exfoliants, or citrus-derived surfactants. Swirl to dissolve.
3
Wipe gently with a soft cloth. Dip a lint-free microfiber cloth or a soft cotton cloth into the soapy water and wring it out so it is damp, not wet. Wipe the amber surface using light, circular motions. For the silver setting, use the same cloth, paying attention to any recessed areas where skin oils may accumulate.
4
Rinse with a clean damp cloth. Use a second cloth dampened with clean water to remove any soap residue. Never hold amber under running water — the stream pressure can stress settings, and soaking can work moisture into micro-pores in the stone.
5
Dry immediately and thoroughly. Pat your piece completely dry with a clean soft cloth. Allow it to air-dry for a further ten minutes before storing or wearing. Residual moisture trapped against the silver setting can accelerate tarnishing.

Re-Oiling: Restoring Amber's Natural Glow

Over time, amber can begin to look slightly dull or feel dry — a sign that it has lost surface moisture to evaporation. A very light application of olive oil or sweet almond oil will restore its depth and luster. Apply a single drop to your fingertip, rub your fingers together to warm the oil, then gently massage it over the amber surface. Leave it for two minutes, then buff away any excess with a dry cloth. The result should be a revived warmth and translucency, not an oily sheen. Do this no more than two or three times per year.

Caring for the Silver Settings

AMBERRA pieces are set in 925 sterling silver or 24k gold-plated silver. Sterling silver will naturally tarnish over time — this is not a defect but a property of the metal reacting with sulfur compounds in the air and on skin. A polishing cloth designed for silver will remove tarnish quickly. For gold-plated pieces, avoid polishing cloths and silver dips entirely, as they can remove the plating. Instead, wipe with a soft damp cloth and dry gently. Never use toothpaste, baking soda paste, or commercial silver dips on settings that also hold amber — the chemicals will damage the stone.

Storage and Everyday Precautions

Store amber away from direct sunlight and sources of heat — a drawer or closed jewelry box is ideal. Keep it separate from harder gemstones and metals that could scratch the surface. Remove your amber jewelry before swimming (chlorinated water is particularly damaging), bathing, applying perfume or hairspray, and before any activity involving chemical contact. When getting dressed, make amber the last thing you put on and the first thing you take off.

Treated with this simple care, an AMBERRA piece will not just survive years of wearing — it will develop the kind of lived-in beauty that only genuine natural materials acquire with age.
