How to Tell Real Balinese Amber from Fake: 5 Simple Tests

December 2025
← Back to Journal

The amber market has a counterfeiting problem. As demand for natural amber jewelry has grown, so has the production of convincing imitations — from young plant resin sold as aged amber, to glass, to plastic molded with artificial "inclusions" to mimic the look of the real thing. If you are shopping for amber jewelry anywhere in the world, knowing how to quickly assess authenticity can save you from paying genuine-amber prices for a synthetic stone.

These five tests range from things you can do with items already in your kitchen to observations that require no equipment at all. None of them require damaging a finished piece. Used together, they give a reliable picture of whether what you are holding is genuinely fossilized tree resin — or something else entirely.

Test 1
The Salt Water Float Test

Mix one part salt with two parts water in a glass until fully dissolved — you need a saturated salt solution. Drop your amber into the glass. Genuine amber floats. It has a specific gravity of approximately 1.05 to 1.10, which is just slightly heavier than fresh water but lighter than saturated salt water (specific gravity around 1.13). Glass sinks immediately. Most plastics also sink, though some lower-density plastics may float too, so this test is best used in combination with others. Copal (immature resin — the most common amber imitation) also floats, but it can be distinguished by other tests below. If your stone sinks in saturated salt water, it is definitively not amber.

Test 2
The UV Light Test

Under ultraviolet light, genuine amber fluoresces. The color of this fluorescence depends on the origin and age of the amber: Baltic amber typically glows blue-white to greenish-blue; Indonesian amber, including Balinese varieties, often produces a warmer blue-green to yellow-green glow. Copal (young resin) will usually fluoresce as well, but with a less vivid, more yellowish response. Glass shows no fluorescence. Plastic may produce a faint whitish glow but lacks the characteristic warmth of true amber fluorescence. A UV torch (widely available and inexpensive) is perhaps the most reliable single-tool test available to the non-specialist buyer. Hold the stone in darkness and observe the response at close range.

Test 3
The Heat and Scent Test

Amber is fossilized resin, and when gently warmed, it releases a faint but distinctive scent — a dry, resinous, slightly piney smell reminiscent of tree sap. You do not need to apply a flame. Simply rub the surface briskly with a cloth to create friction warmth, then bring it quickly to your nose. Genuine amber will produce this warm, organic, forest-like scent. Copal, being less mature resin, smells sweeter and more immediately resinous — almost like fresh pine or vanilla resin. Plastic produces a distinctly chemical smell when warm. Glass produces nothing. This test is particularly useful for distinguishing real amber from copal, which passes the salt water test and may pass the UV test — but smells noticeably different under heat.

Test 4
Look for Natural Inclusions

Natural amber formed in a living ecosystem, and it captured that ecosystem as it hardened. Under magnification — even a phone camera zoomed to maximum, or a simple jeweler's loupe — look carefully at the stone's interior. Genuine amber may contain: tiny bubbles (irregular in shape, not perfectly spherical), fragments of ancient plant matter, traces of bark or wood, flow lines from successive resin deposits, or very occasionally, prehistoric insects. The key word is irregularity. Natural inclusions are asymmetric, varied, and distributed without pattern. In fakes, "inclusions" are often perfectly symmetrical air bubbles in identical sizes, or plastic-molded insect shapes positioned unnaturally. Perfect symmetry and perfect clarity are both suspicious in amber — real stones almost always carry some trace of their organic origin.

Test 5
The Price Reality Check

Genuine Balinese amber set in quality silver or gold-plated silver has real material and labor value. If a piece is priced at a few dollars — or even a few tens of dollars for a substantial stone in metal settings — it almost certainly is not real amber. Natural amber jewelry from reputable Balinese workshops reflects the cost of skilled artisan labor in Ubud, genuine sterling silver, and stones that were individually selected and hand-shaped. Be especially skeptical of mass-produced pieces sold in tourist markets with identical "amber" stones in identical sizes. Real amber stones are each unique in color, clarity, and shape, because no two pieces of ancient fossilized resin are identical.

What Amber Fakes Are Actually Made Of

The most common amber imitation is copal — resin from relatively recent trees, typically between a few hundred and a few tens of thousands of years old. Copal has not undergone the full polymerization process and is chemically distinct from true amber. It is softer, more soluble in acetone, and less stable over time — it will cloud and crack with age in ways that genuine amber does not. Copal is widely sold in Bali and across Southeast Asia as amber, sometimes without deliberate intent to deceive, simply because local terminology conflates the two.

Other common fakes include glass (heavier, colder to the touch, no fluorescence), phenolic resins such as Bakelite (common in vintage pieces), and modern acrylic and polyester resins, which can be remarkably convincing optically but fail every physical test.
