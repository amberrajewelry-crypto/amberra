Balinese Amber Healing Properties: The Science Behind Succinic Acid

March 2026
← Back to Journal

For thousands of years, cultures across Asia and Europe have worn amber not simply as adornment but as medicine — pressed against the skin to ease inflammation, calm the nervous system, and restore a sense of quiet energy. Modern biochemistry is beginning to explain why. At the center of amber's therapeutic story is a single molecule: succinic acid.

What Is Succinic Acid?

Succinic acid (butanedioic acid) is an organic compound produced naturally during the Krebs cycle — the fundamental process by which every living cell generates energy. In fossilized tree resin, succinic acid is locked in concentrations that vary dramatically by origin. Baltic amber, the most studied variety, contains between 3% and 8% succinic acid by weight. Balinese amber, formed from the resin of ancient tropical trees that once carpeted the Indonesian archipelago some 20 to 40 million years ago, carries comparably high concentrations — often reaching the upper range of that spectrum due to the warm, humid conditions that accelerated resin polymerization.

What makes this relevant to jewelry is not the acid itself in isolation, but what happens when amber meets the warmth of human skin.

Warmth-Activated Release: How Amber Works Against Skin

Amber is a surprisingly poor conductor of heat — it warms slowly and retains that warmth far longer than metal. When an AMBERRA piece rests against your wrist, throat, or earlobe, the stone gradually reaches skin temperature and holds it. This sustained, gentle warmth is believed to facilitate the slow transdermal release of microscopic succinic acid compounds from the outermost surface layer of the stone.

"The thermal conductivity of amber is among the lowest of any gemstone — approximately 0.2 W/m·K, compared to 320 W/m·K for copper. This means amber acts almost as an insulator, concentrating warmth at the point of skin contact."

Succinic acid, when absorbed through the skin in trace amounts, is thought to function as an antioxidant and mild anti-inflammatory agent. It has been studied in topical and dietary contexts for its ability to neutralize free radicals and support cellular energy production. While the quantities released from a polished amber stone are small, proponents argue that continuous, long-term skin contact creates a cumulative effect.

Traditional Use in Bali

In Bali, amber has held a sacred place in ceremonial and healing practice for centuries. Balinese healers — known as balian — have long incorporated amber resin into rituals intended to balance the body's energy, or sekala and niskala: the visible and invisible forces that govern well-being. Amber beads were worn by infants to ease teething discomfort, by elders to soothe joint stiffness, and by those seeking clarity during meditation and prayer.

This is not mere superstition. The traditional knowledge that amber held some power when worn directly on skin aligns with what we now understand about succinic acid's bioavailability through dermal pathways. Ancient observation preceded the biochemistry by millennia.

Scientific Context: What Research Tells Us

Succinic acid has been studied in pharmaceutical and dermatological contexts with genuine results. Research published in peer-reviewed journals has identified it as a potent free-radical scavenger and a modulator of inflammatory pathways. In topical formulations, it has demonstrated efficacy in reducing skin inflammation markers. Studies on mitochondrial function have also linked succinic acid to improvements in cellular energy metabolism — which may explain the reported sense of vitality and calm that regular amber wearers describe.

It is important to be clear: wearing amber jewelry is not a medical treatment, and AMBERRA makes no therapeutic claims. What we can say with confidence is that the material science of amber — its thermal properties, its organic chemistry, and its long history of intentional skin contact — is more fascinating than most gemstone lore gives it credit for.
