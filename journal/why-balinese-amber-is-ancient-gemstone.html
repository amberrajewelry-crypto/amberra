Why Balinese Amber Is the World's Most Ancient Organic Gemstone

September 2025
← Back to Journal

Most gemstones begin their existence as minerals — crystalline structures built, atom by atom, from the inorganic materials of the earth's mantle and crust. Diamonds are carbon subjected to unimaginable pressure. Sapphires are aluminum oxide colored by trace elements. Emeralds are beryllium silicates. Each is extraordinary, and each is, in the strictest geological sense, born of chemistry and pressure alone.

Amber is something entirely different. It begins as life itself.

The amber in every AMBERRA piece was once the living resin of an ancient tree — a substance the tree produced to heal its own wounds, protect itself from insects and fungi, and seal the damage of a storm or a broken branch. When that resin fell to the ground, or was encased by bark and soil, an extraordinary process began: over millions of years, volatile compounds evaporated, cross-linking reactions occurred, and the soft, sticky sap slowly transformed into something permanent. The result is fossilized resin — the preserved secretion of a living organism, holding within it the memory of the world in which that tree once grew.

Amber, Pearl, and the Organic Gemstone Family

Amber belongs to a very small family of organic gemstones — materials that derive from living organisms rather than inorganic mineral processes. The most familiar members of this family are pearl (formed within mollusks), coral (the skeletal remains of marine polyps), and amber itself. What unites them is an origin in biology: each carries the residue of life within its structure in ways that purely mineral stones cannot.

This organic origin has profound implications for both the material properties and the spiritual character of these stones. Pearl is formed within the lifetime of a single creature. Coral accumulates over decades or centuries. Amber, however, operates on an entirely different timescale — one measured in tens of millions of years. The amber sourced for AMBERRA pieces is Balinese amber, estimated at between 20 and 40 million years of age. The trees that produced this resin belonged to extinct species, living in forests that no longer exist anywhere on earth. Wearing Balinese amber is, in the most literal sense, wearing a piece of a vanished world.

The Critical Distinction: Copal Versus True Amber

Not all fossilized resin is amber — a distinction that matters enormously to those who care about authenticity. Copal is young resin, typically less than one million years old, that has undergone partial polymerization but has not yet completed the full fossilization process that transforms it into amber. Copal is found widely in tropical regions, including parts of Southeast Asia and Africa, and is frequently sold as amber at significantly lower price points.

The differences between copal and true amber are substantial. Copal is softer and more susceptible to surface dissolution when exposed to solvents. It lacks the complete cross-linking that gives amber its characteristic hardness and stability. Most importantly for those who regard their jewelry as more than decoration, copal has not undergone the full geological transformation that makes true amber what it is — a material that has survived, essentially unchanged, since before the first humans walked the earth. True amber requires at least two million years of geological processing; the most prized specimens are significantly older.

Amber is time made tangible — not the abstract time of clocks and calendars, but deep geological time, the kind that renders human history a brief and recent footnote.

What Makes Amber Rare

Even within the category of genuine amber, significant variation in quality and rarity exists. The factors that determine exceptional amber are clarity, the presence and quality of inclusions, size, and color.

Clarity refers to the degree of transparency in the stone. While all amber varieties have their value, a piece of golden honey amber with near-perfect transparency — through which you can see clearly to the other side — represents a relatively uncommon formation. Most amber contains bubbles, suspended particles, or oxidation layers that reduce transparency.

Inclusions — the creatures, plant materials, or air bubbles preserved within the amber — are perhaps the most scientifically compelling aspect of the stone. Finding an intact insect, a fragment of ancient bark, or even a hair from a Paleogene mammal preserved within amber is extraordinarily rare and extraordinarily significant. Such pieces are not used in jewelry; they belong in natural history collections. But even smaller, more subtle inclusions — tiny air bubbles arranged in ancient patterns, fragments of bark that left ghostly traces — contribute immeasurably to a stone's character and uniqueness.

Size matters too. The tree resin pooled in natural depressions and wounds in the bark, rarely accumulating in large masses. Pieces large enough to be set as significant jewelry stones required unusual conditions of production and preservation, making them inherently less common than small fragments.

Balinese Amber and Its Spiritual Significance

Indonesia has long been a center of amber use in ritual and spiritual practice. Balinese amber, in particular, is embedded in a living tradition that regards the stone not merely as beautiful but as genuinely active — capable of protecting, healing, and transmitting intention. Balinese priests and healers have used amber in ceremony for centuries, regarding it as a conduit between the material world and the spiritual forces that Balinese Hinduism recognizes as shaping all of existence.

They are a genuine continuation of practices that have surrounded this material for generations.

AMBERRA's Verification Process

Because the distinction between genuine ancient amber and copal is not always visible to the naked eye, AMBERRA is rigorous in its sourcing and verification. Our team works with established Balinese amber suppliers who have documented relationships with the specific geological deposits from which material is sourced. Each significant piece undergoes evaluation for hardness, surface behavior, and refractive properties consistent with genuine fossilized resin.

We do not use reconstructed or pressed amber — amber fragments heated and fused into larger pieces — in any of our designs. Every stone in an AMBERRA piece is a single, natural specimen, shaped by nature and set by hand. When you wear AMBERRA, you wear something that existed, in its present form, long before any human hand touched it. That unbroken continuity — from ancient forest to living skin — is what we are committed to preserving.
