The Ubud Artisans: Hands Behind Every AMBERRA Piece

August 2025
← Back to Journal

In a narrow lane off Jalan Hanoman in Ubud, behind a gate hung with frangipani and a small shrine bearing a fresh daily offering of rice, incense, and flowers, a family has been making silver jewelry for four generations. The grandfather learned from his father; the father taught his sons; now the sons teach their own children beside them at the same low workbenches, using tools that in some cases are older than the modern nation of Indonesia. This is the kind of workshop where AMBERRA pieces are born.

Ubud has been a center of Balinese craft for centuries — not merely as a commercial phenomenon but as a spiritual and cultural imperative. In Balinese Hindu tradition, the making of beautiful objects is understood as a form of devotion: an offering to the divine forces that permeate all things. A piece of jewelry made with skill, intention, and proper ritual observance carries a different quality from one produced in a factory. This is not sentiment. It is, within the Balinese worldview, a statement of fact — and it is a belief our artisan partners hold as seriously as any technical specification.

Centuries of Silversmithing in Bali

Balinese silversmithing as a formal tradition reaches back at least to the Majapahit period of the 14th century, when the Hindu-Javanese kingdom extended its influence across the archipelago and brought with it refined metalworking techniques. Over subsequent centuries, Balinese smiths developed a distinctive aesthetic vocabulary: intricate surface ornamentation, floral and zoomorphic motifs drawn from Hindu-Buddhist cosmology, and a particular mastery of working with thin, delicate forms that appear impossibly complex for the tools available.

What distinguishes Balinese silversmithing from other regional traditions is the insistence on handwork at every stage. Balinese silversmiths historically resisted the introduction of casting and machine-pressing techniques that simplified production elsewhere. The result is a craft culture in which the hand remains the primary instrument — and in which the quality of a piece is directly legible in the touch of that hand.

Hand-Carving and Repoussé

AMBERRA's silver settings begin as sheet silver or wire, shaped at the workbench by hand. Decorative surfaces are carved using fine gravers — small steel tools with different tip profiles for different cut qualities. Repoussé work, in which the metal is pushed from behind to create raised surfaces, is used to build dimensional detail around amber settings, creating frames that feel like they have grown organically around the stone rather than been fabricated to contain it.

This approach is time-consuming in ways that resist easy quantification. A single ring setting might require four to six hours of carving work before the amber is placed. A bracelet with a complex repeating motif can take two full working days. The time is not inefficiency — it is the direct expression of value, inscribed in the metal through the patience and skill of a craftsperson who has spent years developing their hand.

Granulation: The Ancient Surface Technique

Among the most distinctive elements of Balinese silverwork is granulation — the application of tiny spheres of silver to a surface, creating texture and pattern through the accumulation of minute forms. Each granule is made individually, formed by melting a small quantity of silver into a ball using a flame. The granules are then arranged on the surface of the piece and fused using a technique that bonds them without solder, through a combination of heat and a copper-salt solution that facilitates fusion at the contact points alone.

Proper granulation, in which each sphere is perfectly round, uniformly sized, and bonded cleanly, is one of the most technically demanding skills in silversmithing. It cannot be rushed or approximated. AMBERRA uses granulation selectively, in pieces where the technique adds genuine dimension to the relationship between silver and amber — where the tiny spheres echo the rounded warmth of the stone itself.

Bezel Setting for Amber

Amber presents specific challenges as a setting stone. Unlike diamonds or sapphires, it cannot withstand the pressure of prong setting without risk of cracking. It is also sensitive to heat, making techniques that involve extended torch work near the stone inadvisable. The bezel setting — in which a continuous wall of silver is formed around the stone's perimeter and pressed inward to secure it — is the traditional and appropriate method, and it is what our artisans use for every AMBERRA piece.

A well-made bezel is not merely functional. It is part of the design: its height, its texture, the way its edge is finished — all contribute to how the amber is perceived. Our artisans calibrate bezels to each individual stone, since no two pieces of natural amber are identical in shape or thickness. This individual fitting is another irreducible element of handcraft — something that cannot be standardized or automated without losing the intimacy between setting and stone.

In Bali, we say that a skilled craftsperson does not simply make an object — they invite a spirit into it. The intention you bring to your work determines what lives there afterward.

Sacred Geometry in AMBERRA Designs

Balinese design is deeply informed by sacred geometry — proportional systems and geometric forms drawn from Hindu-Buddhist cosmological mathematics. The mandala, the lotus, the naga spiral, the tri-kona (triangle): these are not merely decorative shapes. They are structural diagrams of spiritual principles, understood to carry the energetic qualities of what they depict. When an AMBERRA piece incorporates a lotus motif around an amber stone, the intention is not ornamental. The lotus, in Balinese tradition, is the flower of spiritual awakening — it is placing the amber within a field of that particular intention.

Ritual in the Workshop

Every morning, before work begins in an AMBERRA artisan workshop, a brief ritual is performed. Small offerings — canang sari, the square woven-palm trays filled with flowers, incense, and rice — are placed at the workshop shrine and at the workbench itself. A short prayer is spoken. Incense is lit. This is not ceremony for visitors. It is the ordinary daily practice by which a Balinese craftsperson enters a state of aligned intention before touching their materials.

The full moon holds particular significance in Balinese spiritual life, and AMBERRA has built a formal blessing schedule around the lunar cycle. Completed pieces designated for the Sacred Spring Ritual collection are blessed at Pura Tirta Empul, the sacred spring temple in Tampaksiring, on auspicious lunar dates. Full Moon collections receive blessing at the full moon ceremony. These rituals are conducted by Balinese priests and are understood, within the tradition, to permanently alter the spiritual character of the objects involved.

PT Amberra Group Creations and the Artisan Community

AMBERRA operates through PT Amberra Group Creations, a Bali-based entity that manages relationships with the artisan families who produce our pieces. These are not contract manufacturing arrangements — they are genuine partnerships, developed over years, with families whose craft we deeply respect and whose livelihoods we are committed to supporting fairly.

Fair pricing, timely payment, and sustained work through slower seasons are the foundations of these relationships. We are also committed to ensuring that the knowledge held within these families — the techniques, the design vocabulary, the ritual practices — is recognized as having value and is treated accordingly. Every AMBERRA purchase is a direct contribution to the continuation of a living craft tradition that has existed in Ubud for centuries. That is something worth protecting.
